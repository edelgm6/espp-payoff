Valuing the replicating portfolio

Now, we have a replicating portfolio consisting of

  • 150 shares of stock
  • Negative exposure to 150 call options at a strike price of $14.70
  • Positive exposure to 525 call options at a strike price of $28.00
Valuing the stock is straightforward: 150 shares x $28.00 = $4,300. But what about the call options?

The mechanics of option pricing are beyond the scope of this website, but what's most important to grasp is that the higher the volatility, the higher the option value. I.e., an ESPP companies with steady stock prices is lower than that of highly unpredictable stock prices.

To see why this is, consider the chart on the right which shows a call option with a $4 strike price. Because an option has an asymmetric payoff:

  • If you buy a $100 share of Apple and it goes up to $200, you make $100. If it goes down to $0, you lose $100
  • If you buy a call option for Apple stock with a strike price of $100 for $10 and it goes up to $200, you make $100. If it goes to $0, you're only out the $10!
Because of this dynamic, as a call option holder you prefer a stock that is likely to go to $200 or $0 more than a stock is that is likely to go to $110 or $90!

Valuing call options

Diving into the specifics of the Black-Scholes option pricing model is outside the scope of this site. The critical takeaways for our purposes are:

  • The lower the call price is relative to the current price, the higher the value of the option
  • The higher the volatility of the stock, the higher the value of the option
