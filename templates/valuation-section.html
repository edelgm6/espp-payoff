Valuing the replicating portfolio

Now, we have a replicating portfolio consisting of

  • 150 shares of stock
  • Negative exposure to 150 call options at a strike price of $14.70
  • Positive exposure to 525 call options at a strike price of $28.00
Valuing the stock is straightforward: 150 shares x $28.00 = $4,300. But what about the call options?

The mechanics of option pricing are beyond the scope of this website (this tool uses the Black-Scholes model), but what's most important to grasp is that the higher the volatility, the higher the option value. I.e., an ESPP companies with steady stock prices is lower than that of highly unpredictable stock prices.

To see why this is, consider the chart on the right which shows a call option with a $28 strike price. Note that as you adjust the volatility upward, the spread of potential outcomes and the value of the ESPP goes up!

To understand why this is, consider the following:

  • If you buy a $28 share of some stock and it doubles to $56, you make $28. If it goes down to $0, you lose $28
  • If you buy a call option on the stock with a strike price of $28 and it goes up to $58, you make $28. If it goes to $0, you're only out the cost of the option!
Because of this dynamic, as a call option holder you prefer a stock that is volatile to one that is more predictable.
